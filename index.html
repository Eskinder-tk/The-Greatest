Biography

Alexander the Great, born Alexander III of Macedon in 356 BCE, was an ancient king who forged one of the largest empires in world history. Tutored in his youth by the philosopher Aristotle, he inherited the throne of Macedon from his father, Philip II, in 336 BCE. After swiftly consolidating his power by crushing rebellions in Greece, the 20-year-old king launched the ambitious military campaign his father had planned. Possessing a brilliant strategic mind and leading a formidable, battle-hardened army, Alexander set out to conquer the vast Persian Empire, the superpower of the age. Beginning his invasion in 334 BCE, Alexander won a series of decisive victories against the Persian forces led by King Darius III. His conquests stretched from Greece and Egypt all the way to northwest India, creating an empire that fused Greek and Eastern cultures. Along his route, he founded over twenty cities, many named Alexandria, with the one in Egypt becoming a legendary center of learning and culture. His reign, however, was cut short when he died of a fever in Babylon in 323 BCE at the age of 32. Though his empire fractured soon after his death, his conquests initiated the Hellenistic Age, spreading Greek language, art, and ideas across the known world and leaving an indelible mark on the course of history.

Pastimes

Athletics and Physical Exercise

In keeping with the Greek ideal, Alexander valued physical fitness. He was an excellent runner and horseman and encouraged athletic contests and games among his troops to keep morale and fitness high.

Hunting

This was a favorite and traditional pastime for Macedonian royalty. Alexander was an avid and fearless hunter, often pursuing dangerous game like wild boars and even lions. It was considered excellent practice for warfare and a way to display courage and physical prowess

Symposia

For Macedonians, these were more than just parties; they were crucial social events for bonding, political discussion, and celebration. Alexander frequently hosted these elaborate gatherings, which involved heavy drinking, conversation, and entertainment, though his indulgence in alcohol was often excessive.

Philosophy

Having been tutored by Aristotle, Alexander possessed a keen and curious intellect. He enjoyed engaging his companions and advisors in deep discussions about military strategy, geography, philosophy, and the nature of the new lands he was conquering.
